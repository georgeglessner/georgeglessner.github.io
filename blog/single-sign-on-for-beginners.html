Single Sign On for Beginners

by George Glessner

Posted on August 10, 2018 at 10:30 am

I was tasked with implementing single sign on (SSO) for my companyâ€™s intranet as my first major project of my internship. Having no knowledge of the subject matter beforehand, I was pretty clueless. So, I thought I would try to explain in general what single sign on is and how it works.

Single sign on, as the name implies, allows you to access one or more resources (applications) once you are authenticated. Authentication only takes one initial login. Once logged in, you are logged in to any application that is SSO enabled. Single sign on works by sending SAML requests and responses to the service provider and identity provider to authenticate users. The service provider is the application you are trying to access, such as a company website, and the identity provider is the authenticating source (Auth0, Workspace ONE, etc.).

What I mean by this is say you have sites A, B, and C that are all set up with single sign on for your company or organization. You have not logged in to any of the sites yet and you navigate to site B. Site B sends a SAML request which prompts you to log in via an SSO login provided by the identity provider. Once you provide the correct credentials, the identity provider sends a SAML response back to Site B to be verified. Once site B verifies the response, you are granted access to the site.

Now, say you navigate to site A or C. A SAML request is made just like before, but now the identity provider knows that you are authenticated so it can skip the login process and just send the SAML response back to the requesting site. Once the site receives the response, you are granted access!

Single sign on is not only more secure than the classic sending of usernames and passwords via HTML forms (yikes!), but it is more user-friendly in that you only need to log in to one single sign on enabled site to gain access to all of the others.
